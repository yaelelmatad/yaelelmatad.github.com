Small Data

 
 
 
 
 

The last time we looked at the women and men race data we found something peculiar.  That when you looked at curves of age participation for any given race year, you found that the highest percent of women was always in the youngest demographic.  However, If you were carefully looking, you found that if you just shift the curves by the number of years in between them, you can succeed in “collapsing” the data thusly:

That means that women who were 25 years old in 1995 have the same % participation relative to their 25 year old male counterparts in 1995 as 40 year old women in 2010 have to their 40 year old male counterparts.  That suggests women born in 1970 are participating relatively constantly in race year time relative to men born in 1970. 

Let’s look at the same data plotted by birth year. What is the difference between looking at the data by birth year vs. age? People’s age changes in time, people’s birth year does not.  Therefore, if I look at a group by birth year I know that I am roughly looking at the same subset of people.  If I looked at them by age, I would be looking at a different group of 25 years in 1995 than in 2010. 

In the next plot, it is important to note that I’ve cut off data for the birth year larger than 1975 for the 1995 data because those people were 20 or younger in 1995 and generally, across the board, participation before the age of about 20 is very low and therefore extremely noisy.

There’s really nothing new here other than I’ve got you used to to thinking about birth year rather than age.  As excepted, we see relatively the same collapse.  Ok.  Now let’s check if this “constant” ness I’ve been talking about is universal.  Let’s look at the ratio of men to women across several race years for various birth year ranges (I’ve averaged over 3 birth years and 3 race years to make the data a bit less noisy.)  I’ve also cut off race years for which the runners in that range would’ve been less than 20 years old.  Again, because that data is always very minimal and quite noisy.

And here is the point: Whatever the ratio of men to women of the same birth year is when people start running in their early and mid 20s remains that way FOREVER MORE.  Which means women are no more likely to stop running as men are to stop running.  They run, relative to their age-group peers, at the same rate always!  So now that we know that, we can just average over all race years to get a plot of the % Female runners by birth year averaged over all race years (since we know that remains constant.)

Isn’t that awesome?  For people born in the 40s, women account for only 25% of the age group.  For women born in the mid 80s, they account for 60% of the age group!  The dotted line represents the 50% mark.  The crossover between mostly men to mostly women in that age group seems to happen for people born in the mid 1970s.  We have finally solved the mystery of why the youngest age groups are filled with tons of women, but the older ones are not.  Those women just weren’t born in the right year for women’s running!
