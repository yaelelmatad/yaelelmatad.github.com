Small Data

 
 
 
 
 

Sitting down with the data, it was clear that there was something big happening.  Let’s look at something very basic, the sheer number of total finishers (which may include the same person finishing multiple races) in a given race year for races between 1 mile and 26.2 miles (a marathon).

What this shows is that in the past decade there has been a HUGE increase in the number of people finishing NYRR races.  There are three factors I can think of at the moment.  1. Running (in NYC) has become increasingly popular. 2. (corollary  of one?) Due to increased Demand, NYRR has been holding more races. (EDIT: This one doesn’t seem to be true, a cursory look at the race list from the early 90s and late 00s doesn’t seem to show a significant change in number of NYRR events). 3. (corollary of one?) Due to increased demand, NYRR has been making race caps higher. This may also be related to the increased popularity of the 9+1 program where one gains entry into the NYC Marathon by running 9 of the NYRR weekly races.

Now let’s look at those numbers broken down by men and women.

So the Male and Female curves seem to follow roughly the same shape (as the total runners).  The interesting thing is that the difference has basically been constant, even though the total runners has gone from only about 10,000 per year to over 150,000 per year.  The difference between the curves has stayed roughly at around 10,000/per year as shown below (yes it looks noisy, but trust me, it’s about constant):

Okay, well that’s kind of curious but perhaps not very interesting (well it is, but not very insightful).  A better measure of the amount of women is the ratio between the female runners to total runners. Here it is plotted as the percentage of female runners:

Here is where we really start to see something spectacular.  In the late 80s LESS THAN 1 in 4 NYRR race finishers were women.  By 2010, that number is nearly 1 in 2, or even participation between men and women.  The black line here is the fit to a line (For linear regression nerds: the 2nd order polynomial gave a leading coefficient of basically 0, suggesting that the a line is better fit than the parabola.).  This seems to suggest that the growth in the fraction of female runners has been roughly linear in time.

Is that the whole story? Not exactly, there is still that weirdness I pointed out in the first post about the age group discrepancy.  So now the last remaining question: Have the numbers in every age group gone up proportionally or is there something distinctly different about the under 30 year old women and the early 40 year old women?  Moreover, are there some unknown factors which encourage women to drop running after 30? [Sidenote: When I first pointed this out lots of people said to me “They have babies! They get married etc”.  Not very convincing arguments to me personally, but let’s leave that investigation for another post.]
